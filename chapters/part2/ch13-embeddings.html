Chapter 13

Word Embeddings & Representation Learning

How do you feed a word into a neural network? Numbers go in, numbers come out — but words aren’t numbers. The solution is embeddings: learned vector representations that capture meaning. Words with similar meanings land near each other in a high-dimensional space, and the directions in that space encode semantic relationships. If this reminds you of choosing a basis for signal representation, you’re onto the deepest idea in this chapter.

13.1 The One-Hot Problem

The simplest encoding: give each word its own dimension. With a vocabulary of V words, each word becomes a vector of length V with a single 1 and the rest 0s.

“cat” → [0, 0, 1, 0, 0, …, 0]
“dog” → [0, 0, 0, 1, 0, …, 0]
“car” → [0, 0, 0, 0, 1, …, 0]

This seems reasonable until you notice three fatal problems:

Orthogonality

Every word is equally distant from every other. “cat” is no closer to “dog” than to “democracy”. The dot product between any two one-hot vectors is always zero.

Dimensionality

Real vocabularies have 30K–100K words. That means 100K-dimensional vectors that are 99.999% zeros. Massively wasteful.

No Generalization

Learning something about “cat” tells the model nothing about “kitten”. Each word is its own island — no structure to exploit.

For a vocabulary of size V, the one-hot encoding of word i is the standard basis vector:

e_i ∈ ℝ^V,   (e_i)_j = δ_{ij}

⟨e_i, e_j⟩ = 0    for all i ≠ j
||e_i - e_j||² = 2   for all i ≠ j

Every pair of words has identical distance √2. The representation contains zero semantic information — it’s purely an index.

We need something better: a dense, low-dimensional representation where similar words have similar vectors. But how do we discover what “similar” means without hand-engineering thousands of features?

13.2 The Distributional Hypothesis

The answer comes from linguistics:

“You shall know a word by the company it keeps.”
— J.R. Firth, 1957

Words that appear in similar contexts tend to have similar meanings. Consider:

“The cat sat on the mat and purred.”
“The kitten sat on the mat and purred.”
“The dog sat on the mat and barked.”

“Cat” and “kitten” share almost identical contexts. “Dog” shares many but differs in some. “Algorithm” would never appear here. The context is the meaning.

This is a profound insight: we don’t need labeled data or human-curated features. We just need lots of text and a clever way to turn co-occurrence patterns into vectors.

13.3 Word2Vec

In 2013, Mikolov et al. at Google introduced Word2Vec — two simple neural network architectures that turn the distributional hypothesis into dense vector representations.

CBOW (Continuous Bag of Words)

Given the context, predict the center word.

Input: “The ___ sat on” → predict “cat”.

Average the context word embeddings, pass through a single linear layer, softmax over vocabulary. Fast, works well for frequent words.

Skip-Gram

Given the center word, predict the context.

Input: “cat” → predict “The”, “sat”, “on”.

Take center word embedding, predict each context word independently. Slower but works better for rare words and small datasets.

Skip-gram objective — maximize the probability of context words given center word:

J = (1/T) Σ_{t=1}^{T} Σ_{-c ≤ j ≤ c, j≠0} log P(w_{t+j} | w_t)

P(w_O | w_I) = exp(v'_{w_O} · v_{w_I}) / Σ_{w=1}^{V} exp(v'_w · v_{w_I})

Two embedding matrices: W (input embeddings, V×d) and W' (output embeddings, V×d). The softmax denominator sums over the full vocabulary — expensive!

Negative sampling approximation:

log σ(v'_{w_O} · v_{w_I}) + Σ_{k=1}^{K} 𝔼_{w_k ~ P_n(w)} [log σ(-v'_{w_k} · v_{w_I})]

Instead of computing softmax over all V words, sample K negative examples (typically 5–20). Push the target word’s dot product up and random words’ dot products down.

The key insight: the embedding matrix itself is the product. After training, throw away the prediction head. The V×d weight matrix W gives you a d-dimensional vector for every word. Typical dimensions: 100–300.

13.4 Embedding Geometry & Analogies

The most stunning property of word embeddings: directions encode relationships. The famous example:

king − man + woman ≈ queen

This works because the vector from “man” to “king” captures the concept of “royalty,” and adding that direction to “woman” lands near “queen.” The embedding space has learned semantic axes.

Gender axis

king − queen ≈ man − woman ≈ uncle − aunt

Country–Capital axis

Paris − France ≈ Tokyo − Japan ≈ Berlin − Germany

Tense axis

walking − walk ≈ swimming − swim ≈ running − run

Analogy solving — “A is to B as C is to ?”

d = v_B − v_A + v_C

? = argmax_{w ∉ {A,B,C}} cos(v_w, d)

cos(u, v) = (u · v) / (||u|| · ||v||)

Compute the target vector, then find the word whose embedding has the highest cosine similarity to it (excluding the input words).

Why cosine similarity? Euclidean distance is affected by vector magnitude. Cosine similarity measures only the angle between vectors, which better captures semantic direction regardless of word frequency effects on magnitude.

These regularities emerge without any supervision about relationships. The network discovers structure purely from word co-occurrence patterns in text.

13.5 Contextual Embeddings

Word2Vec assigns a single vector per word. But words have multiple meanings:

“I sat by the river bank.”
“I deposited money at the bank.”

Same word, completely different meanings. Static embeddings average all senses into one vector. Modern models solve this with contextual embeddings — the vector for a word depends on the entire sentence around it.

ELMo (2018)

Bi-directional LSTM (Chapter 12!) reads the sentence left-to-right and right-to-left. Concatenate hidden states at each position. Different layers capture syntax vs semantics.

BERT (2018)

Transformer encoder (Chapter 15). Mask random words, predict them from context. Truly bidirectional — every token attends to every other. Revolutionized NLP.

GPT (2018–present)

Transformer decoder. Left-to-right only, but scales massively. Each layer produces richer contextual representations. Foundation of modern LLMs.

The evolution: one-hot (no meaning) → Word2Vec (one meaning per word) → ELMo/BERT/GPT (meaning depends on context). Each step adds more information to the representation.

13.6 Bridge — Embeddings as a Learned Basis

In DSP, we represent signals using a basis: the Fourier transform decomposes a signal into sinusoidal components, each with a coefficient. The choice of basis determines what’s easy to see.

Word embeddings are doing the same thing. The embedding matrix W defines a learned basis for representing words. Each dimension is a “semantic frequency” — not hand-designed like Fourier harmonics but discovered from data.

DSP Concept Embedding Analogue Connection
Basis functions (sin, cos) Embedding dimensions Both provide coordinates in a representation space
Fourier coefficients Embedding values How much of each basis component is present
Change of basis (DFT matrix) Embedding matrix W Both map from one representation to another
Filter bank decomposition Multi-head embeddings Decompose input into parallel sub-representations
Parseval’s theorem (energy preserved) Distance preservation Semantic distances maintained in lower-dimensional space
Mel scale (perceptual warping) Subword tokenization Both warp resolution to match meaningful structure
Fixed basis (DFT) Word2Vec (static) Same decomposition regardless of context
Adaptive filter bank Contextual embeddings (BERT) Basis adapts to the input — context-dependent decomposition

The deepest connection: the Fourier transform is the optimal basis for stationary signals. Word2Vec finds a good basis for language. Contextual models like BERT learn an adaptive basis that changes with context — exactly like the adaptive filters of Chapter 8 adjust their coefficients to the current signal environment.
