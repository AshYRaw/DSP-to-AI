Chapter 06

Poles, Zeros, and Filter Character

Two dots on the complex plane tell you everything about a filter: what it passes, what it blocks, whether it's stable, and how it sounds. Poles and zeros are the DNA of digital systems — and in AI, they become the eigenvalues that control memory and stability in state-space models.

6.1 — What Are Poles and Zeros?

Why should I care?

Poles and zeros are the most compact description of a system. A filter with 1000 coefficients might have just 10 poles and 10 zeros — and those 20 complex numbers tell you everything. In state-space models like Mamba, the eigenvalues of the A matrix are exactly the poles. Understanding pole-zero placement is understanding how memory, stability, and frequency selectivity work — in both classical DSP and modern AI.

Recall from Chapter 5 that a transfer function is a ratio of polynomials:

H(z) = G · (z − z1)(z − z2)… / (z − p1)(z − p2)…
Zeros (zk)

Values of z where H(z) = 0. Roots of the numerator. At these complex frequencies, the system output is zero — these frequencies are blocked.

Symbol: ○ (open circle on z-plane)

Poles (pk)

Values of z where H(z) → ∞. Roots of the denominator. Near these complex frequencies, the system amplifies enormously — these frequencies are boosted.

Symbol: × (cross on z-plane)

Think of poles as mountains and zeros as valleys on a landscape defined over the complex plane. The frequency response is a path along the unit circle through this landscape — it goes up near poles (resonance) and down near zeros (cancellation).

6.2 — The Pole-Zero Plot

The pole-zero plot places all poles (×) and zeros (○) on the complex z-plane, with the unit circle drawn as a reference. This single diagram reveals:

  • Filter type — lowpass, highpass, bandpass, notch, all-pass
  • Stability — all poles inside unit circle = stable
  • FIR vs IIR — poles only at origin = FIR; poles elsewhere = IIR
  • Resonances — poles near unit circle = sharp peaks
  • Nulls — zeros on unit circle = perfect frequency cancellation

For systems with real coefficients (all practical filters), poles and zeros come in conjugate pairs: if p = a + jb is a pole, then p* = a − jb is also a pole. This symmetry about the real axis is enforced in Tool 6.1 below.

6.3 — Reading Frequency Response from the Pole-Zero Plot

There's a beautiful geometric interpretation. At any frequency ω, the magnitude of the frequency response is:

|H(ejω)| = |G| · (product of distances from ejω to zeros) / (product of distances from ejω to poles)

As you walk around the unit circle:

  • When you approach a pole, the denominator distance shrinks, so |H| increases (resonance peak)
  • When you approach a zero, the numerator distance shrinks, so |H| decreases (notch/null)
  • A zero exactly on the unit circle gives |H| = 0 at that frequency — perfect cancellation
  • A pole close to the unit circle gives a sharp, tall peak — the closer, the sharper

Start with the factored transfer function evaluated on the unit circle (z = ejω):

H(ejω) = G · ∏k (ejω − zk) / ∏k (ejω − pk)

Taking absolute values:

|H(ejω)| = |G| · ∏k |ejω − zk| / ∏k |ejω − pk|

Each term |ejω − zk| is literally the Euclidean distance in the complex plane from the point ejω (on the unit circle) to the zero zk.

The phase response follows similarly:

∠H(ejω) = ∑k ∠(ejω − zk) − ∑k ∠(ejω − pk)

Each angle term is the angle of the vector from the pole/zero to the point on the unit circle.

The Designer's Intuition
To boost a frequency: place a pole near the unit circle at that angle.
To block a frequency: place a zero on (or near) the unit circle at that angle.
To make a lowpass: pole near z=1 (DC), zero near z=−1 (Nyquist).
To make a highpass: pole near z=−1, zero near z=1.
To make a bandpass: complex pole pair near unit circle at desired angle, zeros at z=1 and z=−1.
To make a notch: complex zero pair ON unit circle at target frequency, with poles inside for passband recovery.

6.4 — Stability: The Unit Circle Rule

We saw this in Chapter 5, but it's worth repeating with the pole-zero perspective because it's the single most important design constraint:

|p| < 0.9

Well inside unit circle. h[n] decays quickly. Short memory. Wide, gentle frequency peaks.

|p| ≈ 0.95–0.99

Near unit circle. h[n] rings for many samples. Long memory. Sharp, tall frequency peaks. Useful but sensitive.

|p| = 1.0

ON the unit circle. h[n] oscillates forever. Infinite memory. The system is a pure oscillator (marginally stable). An integrator.

|p| > 1.0

OUTSIDE unit circle. h[n] grows exponentially. UNSTABLE. Output explodes. Must never happen in deployed systems.

The angle of a pole determines which frequency is affected. The magnitude determines how strongly — and whether the system is stable. This is a direct analog to the eigenvalue spectrum of recurrent systems in AI.

A single real pole at z = a gives the impulse response:

h[n] = an u[n]

where u[n] is the unit step. The behavior depends entirely on |a|:

  • |a| < 1: exponential decay — 0.9100 ≈ 0.000027. Memory fades.
  • |a| = 1: constant — 1n = 1 forever. Perfect memory.
  • |a| > 1: exponential growth — 1.1100 ≈ 13,781. Explosion.

A complex pole pair at z = re±jθ gives:

h[n] = rn cos(nθ) u[n]

This is a damped sinusoid at frequency θ with decay rate r. When r = 0.95 and θ = π/4, the system rings at π/4 rad/sample with amplitude halving every ~14 samples.

6.5 — Filter Character from Pole-Zero Placement

With the geometric understanding, we can design filters by placing poles and zeros where we want them. Here are the classic patterns:

Lowpass

Pole near z = 1 (boosts DC). Zero near z = −1 (blocks Nyquist). Result: passes low frequencies, attenuates high.

Highpass

Pole near z = −1. Zero near z = 1 (blocks DC). Result: passes high frequencies, removes slow trends.

Bandpass

Complex pole pair at target angle. Zeros at z = 1 and z = −1 (block DC and Nyquist). Result: passes narrow band.

Notch (Band-reject)

Complex zero pair ON unit circle at target frequency. Poles inside at same angle to keep passband flat. Result: removes one specific frequency.

All-Pass

Zeros at 1/p* (reciprocal conjugate of each pole). |H| = 1 everywhere. Only phase changes. Used in phase equalization.

Comb Filter

Evenly spaced poles around the origin. Creates periodic peaks in frequency response. Used in reverb, pitch effects.

6.6 — Poles and Zeros in AI: Eigenvalues Are Poles

This is one of the deepest connections between DSP and AI. In a state-space model (the mathematical foundation of S4, S5, and Mamba), the system is defined by:

h[n+1] = A · h[n] + B · x[n]      y[n] = C · h[n]

The eigenvalues of the A matrix (state transition matrix) are exactly the poles of the system's transfer function. Everything you've learned about poles applies directly:

DSP Filter Poles
  • |pole| controls decay rate
  • Pole angle controls frequency
  • Close to unit circle = long memory
  • Outside unit circle = unstable
  • Designed by engineer
SSM Eigenvalues
  • |λ| controls memory decay rate
  • λ angle controls oscillation frequency
  • Near unit circle = long-range memory
  • Outside unit circle = exploding gradients
  • Learned via backpropagation (or initialized via HiPPO)
Key Insight
The HiPPO initialization for state-space models places eigenvalues at specific locations in the complex plane to maximize memory of past inputs — it's pole placement for neural networks. Mamba's selective mechanism then adjusts these eigenvalues dynamically based on input content, making it a time-varying pole-placement system. This is the essence of the DSP-to-AI bridge.
