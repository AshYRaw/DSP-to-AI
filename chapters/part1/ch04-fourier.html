Chapter 04

Frequency Domain — The Fourier Transform

Every signal — no matter how complex — can be decomposed into a sum of simple sinusoids. The Fourier Transform is the lens that reveals this hidden structure, and it changes everything about how we think about signals, filters, and computation.

4.1 — The Most Powerful Idea in Signal Processing

Why should I care?

The Fourier Transform is everywhere: audio compression (MP3), image formats (JPEG), wireless communication (OFDM in WiFi/5G), speech recognition, medical imaging (MRI), and deep learning (positional encodings in Transformers use sinusoidal functions for exactly the same reason). Understanding frequency-domain thinking is non-negotiable for understanding modern AI architectures.

Here is the central insight of Fourier analysis:

Fourier's Theorem
Any periodic signal can be represented as a sum of sinusoids at different frequencies, amplitudes, and phases. A square wave is really an infinite stack of sine waves. A violin note is a fundamental frequency plus harmonics. Your voice is dozens of frequencies mixed together. The Fourier Transform tells you which frequencies are present and how much of each.

This means there are two equivalent ways to describe any signal:

Time Domain

Amplitude vs. time. What we see on an oscilloscope. How we naturally perceive signals. x[n] tells us the value at each moment.

Frequency Domain

Amplitude vs. frequency. What we see on a spectrum analyzer. X[k] tells us how much of each frequency is present. Reveals hidden structure.

The Fourier Transform converts between these two views. It's a change of basis — from the time basis (sample values) to the frequency basis (sinusoidal components). No information is lost; the transformation is perfectly reversible.

4.2 — The Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)

For discrete signals (which is all we work with in digital systems), the Discrete Fourier Transform converts N time-domain samples into N frequency-domain coefficients:

X[k] = ∑n=0N−1 x[n] · e−j2πkn/N     k = 0, 1, ..., N−1

And the Inverse DFT recovers the original signal:

x[n] = (1/N) ∑k=0N−1 X[k] · ej2πkn/N     n = 0, 1, ..., N−1

The complex exponential e−j2πkn/N is a rotating phasor. By Euler's formula:

e−j2πkn/N = cos(2πkn/N) − j · sin(2πkn/N)

So the DFT is essentially computing the correlation (inner product) between the signal x[n] and each sinusoidal basis function at frequency k. If the signal contains energy at frequency k, the inner product will be large; if not, it will be near zero.

Key parameters:

  • N = number of samples (determines frequency resolution)
  • X[k] = complex number (magnitude + phase at frequency k)
  • Frequency resolution: Δf = fs / N (sample rate / N)
  • X[0] = DC component (average value). X[N/2] = Nyquist frequency.

The DFT is a matrix multiplication: X = F · x, where F is the N×N DFT matrix with entries F[k,n] = e−j2πkn/N. This matrix is unitary (up to a scale factor), which guarantees perfect reconstruction.

4.3 — Magnitude and Phase: The Two Faces of Frequency

Each DFT coefficient X[k] is a complex number with two parts:

Magnitude |X[k]|
|X[k]| = √(Re² + Im²)

How much of frequency k is present. The magnitude spectrum is what most people think of as "the frequency content." It tells you which frequencies dominate.

Phase ∠X[k]
∠X[k] = atan2(Im, Re)

When each frequency component starts. Phase determines the alignment of sinusoids. Change the phases, keep the magnitudes — the signal sounds the same (to human ears) but looks different.

Together, magnitude and phase fully describe each frequency component. The magnitude spectrum is typically plotted as a bar chart (Tool 4.1 below) or as a continuous curve (Tool 4.2).

Bridge to AI
The DFT computes correlations with sinusoidal templates — exactly like attention computes correlations with learned query/key vectors. Both are inner products that measure similarity. The DFT uses fixed sinusoidal bases; attention uses learned bases. This parallel runs deep through everything we'll see in Part III.

4.4 — The FFT: Making It Fast

The DFT requires O(N²) multiplications — for a million-sample signal, that's a trillion operations. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) computes the exact same result in O(N log N) by exploiting symmetry in the DFT matrix.

Why the FFT Matters
The Cooley-Tukey FFT algorithm (1965) is one of the most important algorithms in history. It reduced what took hours to seconds, enabling real-time audio processing, digital communication, medical imaging, and — as we'll see — efficient convolution. Without the FFT, most modern DSP and much of modern AI would be impractical.

The key insight: an N-point DFT can be split into two N/2-point DFTs — one for even-indexed samples, one for odd:

X[k] = E[k] + WNk · O[k]
X[k + N/2] = E[k] − WNk · O[k]

where E[k] is the DFT of even samples, O[k] is the DFT of odd samples, and WN = e−j2π/N is the "twiddle factor."

This halving continues recursively: two N/2 DFTs become four N/4 DFTs, and so on, until we reach 2-point DFTs (which are trivial additions). The total: log2(N) stages, each with N/2 "butterfly" operations → O(N log N) total.

Practical speedup: For N = 1024: DFT needs ~1M operations, FFT needs ~10K. For N = 1M: DFT needs 1012, FFT needs ~20M. That's a 50,000× speedup.

4.5 — The Convolution Theorem: Frequency Domain Multiplication

This is the single most important theorem connecting Chapters 3 and 4:

Convolution in time ↔ Multiplication in frequency
x[n] * h[n]  ↔  X[k] · H[k]

In words: convolving two signals in the time domain is equivalent to multiplying their Fourier transforms element-by-element in the frequency domain.

Why This Changes Everything
Fast convolution: Instead of the O(NM) direct convolution, compute:
  1. FFT of input: X = FFT(x) — O(N log N)
  2. FFT of kernel: H = FFT(h) — O(N log N)
  3. Multiply: Y = X · H — O(N)
  4. Inverse FFT: y = IFFT(Y) — O(N log N)
Total: O(N log N) instead of O(N²). This is how every large convolution is actually computed in practice — including in deep learning frameworks.

Take the DFT of the convolution y[n] = ∑m x[m] h[n−m]:

Y[k] = ∑n (∑m x[m] h[n−m]) e−j2πkn/N

Swap summation order and substitute p = n − m:

= ∑m x[m] e−j2πkm/N · ∑p h[p] e−j2πkp/N
= X[k] · H[k]

The proof works because the complex exponentials are separable: e−j2πk(m+p)/N = e−j2πkm/N · e−j2πkp/N.

Dual result: Multiplication in time ↔ Convolution in frequency. This duality is used in windowing (multiplying by a window in time = convolving its spectrum with your signal's spectrum).

Bridge to AI
The convolution theorem is why S4 (Structured State Spaces for Sequences) can train efficiently: it converts recurrence into convolution mode during training, then applies the FFT. The duality between convolution (parallel, trainable) and recurrence (sequential, fast inference) is the mathematical foundation of Mamba's architecture, which we'll explore in Part III.

4.6 — Spectrograms: Watching Frequencies Change Over Time

A regular Fourier Transform analyzes the entire signal at once, giving you the average frequency content. But what if the frequency content changes over time? (A melody has different notes at different times. Speech moves through vowels and consonants.)

The spectrogram solves this by computing the FFT on short overlapping windows of the signal. The result is a 2D image:

  • Horizontal axis = time
  • Vertical axis = frequency
  • Color intensity = energy at that frequency at that time

There's a fundamental tradeoff:

Short Window (small FFT)

Good time resolution (see when events happen). Poor frequency resolution (frequencies blur together). Like short-term memory.

Long Window (large FFT)

Good frequency resolution (distinguish close frequencies). Poor time resolution (events blur together). Like long-term analysis.

This time-frequency uncertainty is fundamental — you cannot have perfect resolution in both simultaneously. It's related to the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle in physics.

Bridge to AI
Spectrograms (often Mel-scaled) are the standard input representation for speech recognition models. A spectrogram converts 1D audio into a 2D image, which can then be processed by CNNs or Transformers. The frequency decomposition that the Fourier Transform provides is the first processing step in virtually every modern audio AI system.

4.7 — The Fourier Transform: Connecting DSP to AI

The Fourier Transform is more than a signal processing tool — it's a way of thinking that runs through modern AI:

In DSP
  • Decomposes signals into sinusoids
  • Turns convolution into multiplication
  • Enables fast filtering via FFT
  • Spectrograms for time-frequency analysis
  • Fixed sinusoidal basis functions
In AI
  • Positional encodings use sinusoids
  • S4/Mamba uses FFT for fast training
  • Mel spectrograms feed speech models
  • Attention = correlation with learned bases
  • Learned basis functions (embeddings)
Looking Ahead
In Chapter 5, we'll meet the Z-Transform — a generalization of the Fourier Transform that adds a dimension of exponential growth or decay. While the Fourier Transform lives on the unit circle of the complex plane, the Z-Transform covers the entire plane — and this extra dimension is precisely what we need to understand filter stability, poles and zeros, and ultimately the eigenvalue structure of state-space models like Mamba.
