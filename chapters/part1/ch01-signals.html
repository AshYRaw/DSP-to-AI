Chapter 01

What Is a Signal?

Before we can process signals or build AI systems that understand them, we need to know what a signal actually is — and how a computer captures one.

1.1 — Signals Are Everywhere

Why should I care?

Every piece of data that changes over time or space is a signal. The audio you hear, the images you see, your heartbeat on a monitor, stock prices ticking by — all signals. Understanding signals is step one to understanding how AI models like Transformers and Mamba process the world.

A signal is any quantity that varies as a function of some independent variable — usually time, but it could be space, frequency, or something else entirely.

Think of it this way: if you can draw it on a graph with an axis, it's a signal.

  • Audio — air pressure varying over time
  • Images — brightness varying over 2D space (pixels)
  • EKG — electrical voltage from your heart over time
  • Stock prices — value varying over trading days
  • Text — a sequence of tokens varying over position (yes, this is a signal too!)
Bridge to AI
When GPT processes text or Mamba processes audio, they are processing signals — sequences of numbers that change over time or position. Every AI model, at its core, is a signal processing system.

1.2 — Continuous vs Discrete Time

In the physical world, signals are continuous — sound pressure varies smoothly, never "jumping" between values. We write continuous signals as x(t) where t is continuous time.

But computers can't store infinitely smooth curves. They take snapshots at regular intervals, creating a discrete-time signal: a sequence of numbers. We write this as x[n] where n = 0, 1, 2, ... is the sample index.

This conversion from continuous to discrete is called sampling, and it's one of the most important ideas in all of signal processing.

x[n] = x(nTs)

where Ts is the sampling period (time between samples) and fs = 1/Ts is the sampling rate (samples per second, measured in Hz).

Common sampling rates:

  • 8,000 Hz — telephone
  • 44,100 Hz — CD audio
  • 48,000 Hz — professional audio
  • 96,000 Hz — high-resolution audio

1.3 — Sampling & the Nyquist Theorem

Here's the critical question: how fast do we need to sample to capture a signal faithfully?

If we sample too slowly, we lose information. Worse, we get aliasing — the discrete samples look like they came from a completely different, lower-frequency signal. It's like watching a helicopter blade spin under a strobe light — it appears to spin slowly or even backward.

The Nyquist-Shannon Theorem
To perfectly capture a signal with maximum frequency fmax, you must sample at a rate of at least 2 × fmax. This minimum rate is called the Nyquist rate.

Below the Nyquist rate, high frequencies "fold" back and masquerade as lower frequencies. This is aliasing, and once it happens, you cannot undo it — the information is permanently corrupted.

fs ≥ 2 · fmax

The Nyquist frequency is half the sampling rate:

fNyquist = fs / 2

Any signal component above fNyquist gets "folded" to a frequency below it. Specifically, a frequency f that exceeds fNyquist appears as:

falias = |fs − f|   (when fs/2 < f < fs)

This is why CD audio (44.1 kHz) can capture up to 22.05 kHz — just above the range of human hearing (20 kHz).

1.4 — Common Signal Types

Before we dive into the interactive tools, here are the fundamental building blocks you'll encounter everywhere in DSP and AI:

Sinusoid

The most fundamental signal. Pure tone. Building block of all others (Fourier's insight). Defined by frequency, amplitude, and phase.

Square Wave

Switches between +A and −A. Rich in odd harmonics. Used in digital electronics. Made of infinitely many sinusoids.

Impulse

A single spike at one instant, zero everywhere else. The "ping" that reveals a system's complete character (Chapter 2).

White Noise

Random values at every sample. Equal energy at all frequencies. Sounds like static. Essential test signal.

Chirp

Frequency sweeps from low to high (or high to low). Used in radar (the ancestor of attention — Chapter 17).

Triangle / Sawtooth

Linear ramps create harsh, bright tones. Rich in harmonics. Common in synthesizers.

⚠ Tool 1.2 — Sampling & Aliasing Demonstrator Interactive

The cyan wave is the original signal. The orange dots are what the computer captures. When the sample rate drops below 2× the signal frequency, the dots trace out a red ghost signal — that's aliasing.

Try this

4. Press "Original" then "Aliased" to HEAR the difference. The computer hears a different pitch!
5. Now increase Sample Rate to 40+ Hz — aliasing disappears because Nyquist is satisfied again.
Looking Ahead
Now you know what signals are and how computers capture them. In Chapter 2, we'll learn about systems that process these signals — the first step toward understanding how neural networks transform their inputs. Every layer in a neural network is, mathematically, a signal processing system.
