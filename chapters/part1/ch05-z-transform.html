Chapter 05

The Z-Transform and Transfer Functions

The Fourier Transform shows you the frequency content of a signal. The Z-Transform goes further — it reveals the complete mathematical structure of a system: its stability, its poles and zeros, and its transfer function. It's the engineer's X-ray machine for filters and systems.

5.1 — Why We Need More Than Fourier

Why should I care?

The Z-Transform is the bridge from signals to systems. While the Fourier Transform analyzes signals (what frequencies are present?), the Z-Transform analyzes systems (what does the system do to all possible inputs?). In AI terms: the Z-Transform describes the complete behavior of a layer, not just its response to one input. It's the key to understanding why some systems are stable and others explode, why IIR filters are efficient, and how state-space models in Mamba actually work.

The Fourier Transform has a limitation: it only tells you what happens at frequencies on the unit circle of the complex plane. But system behavior is determined by what happens everywhere in the complex plane — including where the system blows up (poles) and where it goes to zero (zeros).

The Z-Transform fills this gap. It maps a discrete-time sequence to a function of a complex variable z, giving us a complete picture of the system.

5.2 — The Z-Transform: Definition

The Z-Transform of a sequence x[n] is:

X(z) = ∑n=−∞∞ x[n] · z−n

where z is a complex number: z = rejω with magnitude r and angle ω.

Think of z−1 as the delay operator: multiplying by z−1 means "delay by one sample." This makes the Z-Transform a natural language for describing systems that involve delays and feedback.

The Key Relationship
When |z| = 1 (on the unit circle), the Z-Transform reduces to the Fourier Transform:
z = ejω → X(ejω) = DTFT of x[n]

The Fourier Transform is a special case of the Z-Transform, evaluated on the unit circle. The Z-Transform gives you the full picture; the Fourier Transform is a slice of it.

Some fundamental Z-Transform pairs:

δ[n] ↔ 1
Unit impulse maps to constant
δ[n−k] ↔ z−k
Delayed impulse maps to z−k
anu[n] ↔ z/(z−a)
Exponential maps to first-order pole
nu[n] ↔ z/(z−1)²
Ramp maps to double pole at z=1

Key property: The time shift property says x[n−k] ↔ z−kX(z). This means delay in time = multiplication by z−k in the Z-domain. This is why z−1 represents a unit delay.

5.3 — Transfer Functions: H(z)

For an LTI system with impulse response h[n], the Z-Transform H(z) is called the transfer function:

H(z) = ∑n h[n] · z−n = Y(z) / X(z)

The transfer function is the ratio of output to input in the Z-domain. It encodes everything about what the system does. Most practical systems have transfer functions that are rational — ratios of polynomials in z−1:

H(z) = (b0 + b1z−1 + b2z−2 + ...) / (1 + a1z−1 + a2z−2 + ...)

The b coefficients form the numerator (feedforward path) and the a coefficients form the denominator (feedback path).

FIR Systems

Denominator = 1 (no feedback). H(z) is just a polynomial. Only zeros, no poles (except at z=0). Always stable. Finite impulse response.

H(z) = b0 + b1z−1 + ... + bMz−M
IIR Systems

Denominator ≠ 1 (feedback). H(z) has poles from the denominator roots. Can be unstable if poles escape unit circle. Infinite impulse response.

H(z) = B(z) / A(z)

Consider the difference equation:

y[n] = b0x[n] + b1x[n−1] − a1y[n−1]

Take the Z-Transform of both sides, using the shift property x[n−k] ↔ z−kX(z):

Y(z) = b0X(z) + b1z−1X(z) − a1z−1Y(z)

Collect terms:

Y(z)(1 + a1z−1) = X(z)(b0 + b1z−1)
H(z) = Y(z)/X(z) = (b0 + b1z−1) / (1 + a1z−1)

Every linear constant-coefficient difference equation maps directly to a rational transfer function. The Z-Transform turns recursive computation into algebraic division.

5.4 — Region of Convergence: Where H(z) Is Valid

The Z-Transform doesn't converge everywhere in the z-plane. The Region of Convergence (ROC) is the set of z values where the sum converges to a finite value.

For causal systems (the only kind we build in practice), the ROC is the region outside the outermost pole. This leads to a fundamental stability criterion:

The Stability Rule
A causal LTI system is stable if and only if all its poles lie strictly inside the unit circle (|z| < 1). If any pole reaches or exceeds the unit circle, the impulse response grows without bound and the system is unstable.

This is why the z-plane diagram is so important: a single glance at the pole locations tells you whether the system is stable, marginally stable, or unstable.

|pole| < 1

Stable. h[n] decays exponentially. The farther inside the unit circle, the faster the decay.

|pole| = 1

Marginally stable. h[n] oscillates forever without decaying (like a pure sinusoid). An integrator has a pole at z=1.

|pole| > 1

Unstable! h[n] grows exponentially. The system's output explodes. In AI: exploding gradients.

5.5 — The Unit Circle: Where Fourier Lives

The unit circle |z| = 1 has special significance: it's where the Z-Transform becomes the Fourier Transform. Walking around the unit circle from angle 0 to π traces out the frequency response from DC to Nyquist.

H(ejω) = H(z)|z=ejω = frequency response

This means:

  • A pole near the unit circle creates a peak in the frequency response at that angle — the system resonates at that frequency.
  • A zero on the unit circle creates a null — the system completely blocks that frequency.
  • The magnitude of H(z) at any point on the unit circle is the gain at that frequency.

5.6 — The Z-Transform in AI

The Z-Transform might seem like pure classical DSP, but it's directly relevant to modern AI architectures:

In DSP
  • Poles = natural modes of the system
  • |pole| < 1 = stable (decaying memory)
  • Pole angle = resonant frequency
  • Transfer function = system behavior
  • z−1 = one-sample delay
In State-Space Models (Mamba)
  • Eigenvalues of A = poles of the system
  • |λ| < 1 = stable (fading memory)
  • λ angle = oscillation frequency
  • State-space form ↔ transfer function
  • A matrix = state transition (delay + mix)
Bridge to AI
In state-space models like S4 and Mamba, the eigenvalues of the A matrix are exactly the poles of the corresponding transfer function. Stability, memory length, and oscillatory behavior are all determined by where these eigenvalues sit in the complex plane — the same z-plane you see in Tool 5.1. The HiPPO initialization places eigenvalues at specific locations to maximize memory of past inputs. Understanding the z-plane is understanding SSM dynamics.
